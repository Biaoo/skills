# How to Handle Feedback Issues

This document is for Agents who handle skill feedback in this project. It does not prescribe how issues should be triaged, answered, or closed. It sets out the judgments that should guide that work.

Many skills in this project grow out of ongoing thinking about how to work with AI. When Agents carry those ideas into concrete tasks, the ideas shape actions and results; a feedback issue then brings that experience, together with the Agent's interpretation of it, back into the project. It is not merely a proposed change waiting to be accepted or rejected. It is also a trace left by real use.

Every feedback issue is rooted in a concrete case. The user's goal, context, tools, constraints, and the judgments the Agent made in the moment all influence what problem the feedback ultimately reports and what lesson it draws. The issue may identify a real flaw in the skill, or something that matters only in that case; it may expose a gap in the original design, or attribute a local difficulty to the skill. The concrete case deserves serious attention, but it must not become a general rule on its own. Otherwise, skills can overfit the cases that happened to be reported: local rules grown from a handful of cases gradually crowd out the judgment the skill was meant to embody, until the design collapses around a narrow slice of experience. Conversely, design intent must not become a reason to dismiss disagreement.

A feedback conclusion may be right or wrong, but correctness is not the whole of its value. If feedback arising from skill use ends with a view that conflicts with the skill—or is plainly mistaken—one thing is still established: during use or later retelling, an Agent came to understand it that way. Do not stop at “the feedback is wrong.” Ask what mechanisms and conditions could have led the Agent to that understanding. The explanation may lie in the skill's wording or design, the Agent's own tendencies, task pressure, context, tools, or changes introduced while the feedback was relayed. The explanation the Agent gives in the issue must also be evaluated; it is not automatically the actual cause.

These questions are not a way to justify adopting every mistaken suggestion. They are a way to avoid discarding a valuable sample of AI behavior. Many skills in this project are themselves expressions of how to work with AI; feedback shows how Agents actually respond when those ideas meet real tasks. It may lead to a skill change, a clearer boundary, a reconsideration of an existing principle, or only a change in the project's understanding of AI. A careful review may also reasonably end with no change at all.

> Do not let a concrete case grow directly into a rule for a skill. And do not let a mistaken conclusion make you overlook the Agent behavior it reveals—or the question of why the Agent came to think that way.
